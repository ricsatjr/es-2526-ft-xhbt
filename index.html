The image shows a mangrove area with a murky brownish-green coloured and calm water near by. The water seems to be moving in one direction with movements a bit more uniformed, almost pattern like. These mangroves also feature a type of water that we learned about in ES. It's called brackish water, it features a green or brownish colour. Brackish water is a mix of saltwater and freshwater. It forms naturally in estuaries, the meeting point of saltwater and freshwater, and coastal aquifers. Though, it can also be a result of human activities such as excessive groundwater pumping. Its salinity can change daily due to tides, winds, or currents. Brackish water also supports unique ecosystems, having calmer currents and more undisturbed compared to other water. This makes it more possible for organisms to live or inhabit this type of water. At first glance, the image has a clear desire path leading toward the water. Mangroves and their aerial roots stand on either side, while the narrow trail cuts directly through them. To us, this path represents how nature records our presence. It's a trace left behind by repeated human movement. It could be people drawn toward the water, seeking curiosity, discovery, or calmness. The path carries meaning because it exists only through use; it shows how we reshape nature in subtle ways.

There are many fossils in the dirt (Shells and corals mainly). Near the top there is a faint change of color signalling a different layer. 1. Weathering and Erosion: The dirt had been broken down due to weathering, exposing the fossils of corals and other marine life inside of it. While erosion had transported these broken down pieces (sediments) by agents like water, wind, and or gravity, after which they became a part of the soil and dirt in a new location. 2. Principle of Inclusions: The fossils of corals and other marine life have become inclusions within the dirt/soil, showing that these organisms existed before the surrounding sediments were deposited. 3. Principle of Faunal Succession: The fossils of corals and other marine life helped determine the relative ages of rock layers using the principle of faunal succession. “That which is buried will eventually be revealed.” Just like fossils of marine life that were once buried in the soil, they were uncovered over time, helping us understand the history of the Earth.
