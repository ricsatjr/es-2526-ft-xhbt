We're looking straight into the past here. This isn't just dirt and rocks; it's an ancient graveyard of sea life, locked away for a long period of time. The process that makes these remains last so long often involves permineralization. Those white bits are old shells and skeletal fragments from creatures like clams, snails, and corals. When they died, they were covered by mud and sand. Over time, that pressure turned the mud to rock. Water full of dissolved minerals seeped into every gap in the sediment and the shells. The minerals filled those empty spaces, making them solid rock. That's how something becomes a permineralized fossil. Now that the site is exposed to air, it's falling apart. Rainwater is slightly acidic, slowly dissolving those shells. That chalky, pitted look is natural weathering in action, breaking down the fossils piece by piece. These incredible permineralized fossils tell a story of a former ocean, now slowly being worn away by the same natural processes that helped preserve them. This photo showcases several of our quarter two lessons in ES and Biology that we have previously learned and seen during the field trip. It features the concept of relative dating in our ES lesson and the concept of fossils in Biology. We could see the different layers of rocks in the annotated photo, which shows relative dating principles such as the principle of superposition, in which a sequence of rock layers could be seen, and the principle of vertical succession, where fossils could be seen in said layers, each getting progressively older.
